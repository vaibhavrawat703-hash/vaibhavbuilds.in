Warehouse Management System Redesign

Owned the redesign of a WMS around 7 real user personas instead of a generic feature list — restructuring inbound, putaway, picking, packing, and billing around actual operator workflows.

The starting point wasn't a feature backlog — it was 8 problem statements pulled directly from how warehouse staff actually described their day going wrong: misplaced inventory, picking errors under time pressure, packing mistakes that surfaced only at the customer's doorstep. Personas were built around roles that don't get much product attention (the putaway operator, the packer working against a dispatch cutoff), not just the roles that are easy to design for.

Made the case for AI-agent-assisted task execution in picking/packing, prioritizing it over a simpler manual SOP update — the SOP fix would have addressed the symptom (errors), while the agent-assisted flow addressed the root cause (operators making judgment calls under time pressure with incomplete information).

Real-Time Ops Dashboard for Account Managers

Owned the product definition for a dashboard replacing a workflow that ran entirely through phone calls to ops teams.

Account managers had no direct visibility — every status check, every exception, meant calling an ops team member and waiting. The instinct from stakeholders was to build a full action-capable tool (approve, reassign, escalate, all in-app). I deliberately scoped it down to visibility-only instead: account managers needed to see status accurately and immediately, not take action inside the tool — the actions themselves still needed the ops team's context to execute safely.

That scope call kept the build lean, shipped faster, and matched how the team actually worked day to day rather than how a fully-featured tool "should" look on paper.

Doorstep Returns Collection — New Product Line

Owned the 0-to-1 launch of a returns collection service, taking it from concept through pilot.

Early conversations assumed the hard part would be logistics — routing a courier to a customer's doorstep. It wasn't. The real failure mode showed up in disputes over item condition: a customer says the item is fine, the courier or warehouse says otherwise, and there was no clean way to resolve it. That became the actual design center of the product — a structured evidence-and-exception flow captured at the point of pickup, not bolted on after launch once disputes started piling up.

Took the product through a pilot phase to validate that the exception flow held up under real-world ambiguity before wider rollout.
